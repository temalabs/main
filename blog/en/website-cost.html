What Does a Website Really Cost for a UK Café or Restaurant in 2026?

Not from a technical angle, but from a business owner's perspective — no jargon, just clear numbers.

06.21.2026 · TEMA.LABS · 5 min read

Why prices look so chaotic

Most business owners searching "website price" run into this picture:

£300template builder
£750professional build
£2000agency pricing
£5000custom project

Which makes it hard to tell what a website should actually cost. Many café and restaurant owners, seeing this kind of price spread, land on one of two conclusions: either "a website is a luxury I can't afford," or "the cheapest option is the only sensible one." Both conclusions are wrong. The answer depends on what you're really buying, for what purpose, and at what stage your business is at. Let's walk through it step by step, without the jargon.

What determines the price

Price mainly depends on the type and complexity of the site — not whether the developer is "good" or "bad." Two developers with the same experience can quote completely different numbers for what looks like the same job, simply because one client wants a simple business card site and the other needs a full system that takes orders. Here are the most common website types small and medium businesses in the UK run into:

📄

One-page site

A single screen with basic info — the simplest and cheapest option, ideal for getting online fast.

🛠️

Service business site

Multiple sections: about, services, contact, reviews — fits hairdressers, tradespeople, consultants.

🍽️

Restaurant site

Menu, location, booking or ordering capability — the most requested option in the HoReCa space.

🛒

Online store

Product catalog, cart, payment processing — the most complex and expensive option on this list.

Separately, there's custom development — a fully unique design and feature set built around one specific business, which naturally costs more regardless of niche, because every element is built from scratch instead of adapted from an existing structure.

When £2000–5000 isn't worth it

A higher price is justified when a business genuinely needs complex functionality — a large product catalog with hundreds of items, a complex online payment integration tied to accounting software, a multi-level booking system with resource scheduling, or a custom internal CRM. That's the market where large agencies and multi-person teams operate.

But most local businesses — cafés, restaurants, takeaways, hair salons, beauty studios — only need a simple, fast, convenient website that solves one specific job: show who you are, let customers see your menu, and let them reach out or place an order without paying Deliveroo commission. Paying for features that will never be used — a multilingual CMS for a business that only works locally, or a complex admin panel nobody opens more than once a month — just means overpaying for what could be called "digital prestige" rather than real value.

BOTTOM LINE

For most cafés, restaurants and local businesses, a website in the £400–£750 range is enough.
